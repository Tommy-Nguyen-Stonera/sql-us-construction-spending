Data Cleaning & Wrangling

Research Question
How messy is real marketplace data, and what decisions did cleanup force me to make?
analysis.py — Data Cleaning
# Price: force numeric (some rows had currency symbols)
df["sales_price"] = pd.to_numeric(df["sales_price"], errors="coerce")

# Discount: strip % symbol, convert to float
df["discount_pct"] = (
    df["discount_percentage"].astype(str)
    .str.replace("%", "")
    .pipe(pd.to_numeric, errors="coerce")
)

# Category: extract from nested dict
def extract_subcategory(val):
    if not isinstance(val, dict): return "Uncategorised"
    keys = [k for k in val.keys() if k != "HomeImprovement"]
    return keys[0] if keys else "General Home Improvement"

The dataset arrived as line-delimited JSON with prices stored as strings (including currency symbols), discounts as percentage strings, and product categories buried in nested dictionaries. About 8% of products had missing discount percentages, and roughly 200 had no ratings at all.

Where I Got Stuck

The category extraction was the most frustrating part. The parent___child_category__all column contained nested Python dictionaries with varying depth. My first approach — df["category"].str.get("child") — failed because the column contained actual dict objects, not JSON strings. Pandas' string accessor does not work on dicts. I had to write a custom function and use .apply(). The deeper issue was that the nesting was inconsistent: some products had three levels of categorisation, others had one. I ended up grabbing any key that was not "HomeImprovement" and using it as the subcategory. Imperfect, but functional for 145 categories.

Key Decision

I kept products with missing discounts (they likely were not discounted) but excluded unrated products from the rating analysis. A product with zero ratings is fundamentally different from one rated 1 star. That decision affected the rating distribution — including zero-rating products would have pulled the average down and created a different story. Every cleaning decision is an analytical decision.

Key Finding

The market is incredibly fragmented. In physical retail, a few major brands command significant shelf space. Online, the "shelf" is infinite, so the barrier to listing is near zero. The long tail is enormous: hundreds of brands with fewer than 5 listings each. Volume does not guarantee reputation — Stanley and Taparia earn higher ratings with fewer products.

What I Expected vs What I Found

I expected big brands to dominate — like how Makita, Bosch, and DeWalt own the tool section in any physical store. Online, the top brand holds barely 3%. This was so different from physical retail that I had to ask why. The answer is structural: in a showroom, shelf space is finite, so each brand has real power. Online, the constraint is discoverability, not space. But fragmentation creates its own problem — for safety-critical categories like power tools, how does a buyer distinguish between a reputable brand and an unknown? Name recognition still matters for trust-dependent purchases.

Discount Effectiveness

Research Question
Do higher discounts drive more customer engagement? Is there a ceiling beyond which deeper discounts stop working?
analysis.py — Chart 3
# Bin discounts for analysis
df_disc["discount_bin"] = pd.cut(

    labels=["1-10%", "11-20%", ..., "81-100%"]

The average discount is 42% — heavy discounting is standard, not exceptional. Products with 50%+ discounts attract 35% more reviews (median 23 vs 17 for lower discounts). But the relationship is not linear. Beyond roughly 60% off, engagement plateaus.

Key Finding

Discounts work, but there is a ceiling. The 50–60% range is the sweet spot for driving engagement. Beyond 60%, buyers appear to question either the original price or the product quality. This aligns with what I see in-store: customers become suspicious of discounts that look too steep. The 42% average suggests "discount" is partly theatrical — similar to perpetual "sale" pricing in Australian retail.

The Pivot Moment

I initially assumed a straightforward linear relationship: bigger discount equals more sales. The data partially confirmed this — up to a point. When I pushed the analysis past 60%, engagement plateaued. That made me rethink the question entirely.

Average rating: 4.13 out of 5.0. The quality bar is high — low-rated products get buried by the algorithm quickly. The distribution is left-skewed: most products sit between 3.5 and 4.5. Anything below 3.0 is functionally invisible on the platform.

Key Finding

The rating floor is 3.5, not 3.0. Products below 3.5 stars are so rare in this dataset that they effectively do not exist in the marketplace. The algorithm and customer behaviour create a survival bias where only well-rated products remain visible. Safety & Security and Kitchen & Bath Fixtures rate highest, suggesting customers in these categories are more satisfied — or more selective about what they buy.

Where I Got Stuck

The histogram initially looked fine with 30 bins, but the distribution had a suspicious spike at exactly 4.0. I spent time investigating whether Amazon rounds ratings or if this was a data artefact. It turns out many products with few reviews naturally cluster at round numbers (one 4-star review = exactly 4.0). Reducing to 20 bins smoothed this artefact without hiding the overall shape. The lesson: bin count in histograms is an editorial choice, and the default is rarely the right one.

Key Finding

Prime is a price-competition channel. Non-Prime is where margin and specialisation live. This mirrors the in-store vs online split I see in Australian retail: the warehouse store competes on price and convenience, the specialist showroom competes on expertise and quality. The slight rating advantage for non-Prime may reflect better-matched buyer expectations rather than objectively better products.

What I Expected vs What I Found

I assumed Prime = better experience = higher ratings. The opposite is true. Before concluding that non-Prime is "better quality," I had to consider whether the rating difference is driven by product mix rather than fulfilment type. Prime attracts lower-priced commodity items where buyers have low expectations.

Key Finding

At 15.8% concentration, this market is wide open. Compare that to physical retail where the top 3–4 distributors might control 60%+ of supply. Online marketplaces democratise access — but they also commoditise it. Competition is broad but shallow: many sellers with small catalogues rather than a few giants blocking entry.

Business Implication

For someone considering entering this market, the data suggests low barriers to entry but fierce price competition (especially in Prime). The winning strategy appears to be niche specialisation with strong ratings, not catalogue breadth. From my years in building materials, I know the same dynamic plays out in physical wholesale: the generalist competes on price, the specialist competes on expertise and relationship. The marketplace data confirms this pattern extends to online retail.

My Thinking Process & Reflections

Research Question
What went wrong, what changed my approach mid-analysis, and what would I do differently with more time?
Retrospective

The biggest Pandas challenge: The .pipe() method for chaining transformations was new to me. I started with a multi-line approach — df["discount_percentage"].astype(str), then .str.replace("%", ""), then pd.to_numeric(). Chaining these with .pipe() was cleaner, but I initially got confused about which function signature .pipe() expects. The key insight: .pipe(func, *args) passes the Series as the first argument to func. Once I understood that, the cleanup code became a single readable chain instead of three separate assignments.

Matplotlib's default colours made the charts unreadable. The default colour cycle worked fine for the first few charts, but the dual-axis brand chart (bars + line) needed manual colour selection. Using sns.color_palette("muted")[0] for bars and [3] for the line gave enough contrast. I also had to set ylim(2.5, 5.2) on the rating axis — without it, Matplotlib auto-scaled to the full 0–5 range, which compressed the actual variation into a narrow band at the top and made all brands look identical.

The scatter plot that nearly ruined my discount analysis: My first version of the discount-vs-reviews scatter had 4,000+ dots on a linear scale. It was an unreadable blob with a few outliers. I tried reducing alpha to 0.1, which helped but still hid the pattern. The real fix was switching to a log scale on the y-axis (ax.set_yscale("log")) and sampling 1,000 points (df.sample(1000, random_state=42)). The log scale spread out the compressed points, and sampling reduced overplotting. But I had to set random_state to make the chart reproducible — without it, every run produced a different-looking scatter, which made debugging impossible.

Honest Self-Critique

Looking back, I should have explored the "best seller" tag more thoroughly — the dataset includes a best_seller flag that I mentioned but did not fully analyse. What separates best sellers from the rest? Is it price, rating, discount, brand, or some combination? That analysis would have been the most commercially actionable part of the report.

What I Would Investigate Next

Cross-market comparison: Running the same analysis on Amazon Australia or US data would test whether these patterns are India-specific or universal. Price elasticity modelling: With granular sales data, estimate how sensitive demand is to price changes per category. Best seller deep-dive: What separates the products that earn the best seller tag? Review text analysis: NLP on review content could surface specific product strengths and complaints that ratings alone cannot reveal.
